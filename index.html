The Nautilus design solves a deceptively hard problem: how do you design a basin wall that guarantees a shallow impact angle regardless of where the stream hits ‚Äî left, right, off-centre, tall user, short user?

The answer is the logarithmic spiral (r = B¬∑exp(Œ±/tan Œ∏ÃÇ)). This curve appears throughout nature ‚Äî nautilus shells, sunflower seeds, fern fronds ‚Äî because it has a unique property: it maintains a constant angle with any line drawn from its centre. Applied to a urinal, every ray of fluid from any direction always meets the wall at the same acute, splash-preventing angle.

Unlike a flat or simply curved wall, which only works correctly from one specific position, the logarithmic spiral is forgiving by design. A user who is off-centre, slightly turned, or at an unusual height still hits the correct impact zone every time.

Critical secondary benefit: the Nautilus allows a lower lip height than traditional urinals. Traditional designs must sit high to catch the stream ‚Äî but the spiral's curvature means protection starts closer to floor level, making it natively accessible to children and wheelchair users without compromise.

The Cornucopia design solves the vertical plane: from above, a stream falls in a parabolic arc governed by gravity and bladder pressure. Its angle of approach relative to the basin wall changes depending on user height and distance. A standard flat wall presents a near-perpendicular surface to almost all trajectories ‚Äî guaranteeing splashback.

The Cornucopia applies the isogonal curve problem to the side-view (r‚Äìz plane) cross-section. This technique defines a surface that intersects an entire family of parabolic curves ‚Äî every possible urine trajectory ‚Äî at a constant, acute angle of 30¬∞ or less.

The resulting profile, revolved around the vertical axis, produces a horn-like shape: deep, curved, with high swept side walls. This does two things: it redirects the primary stream downward rather than reflecting it, and the high walls catch any satellite droplets before they escape.

In controlled laboratory testing using high-speed imaging, the Cornucopia geometry alone reduced measurable splashback to just 1.4% of a standard commercial urinal ‚Äî before any surface coating is applied.

Why 30¬∞ Is The Critical Number

Below 30¬∞, the stream transitions from reflection mode to redirection mode. Kinetic energy transfers along the surface downward to the drain instead of back toward the user. Satellite droplet formation essentially ceases.

Above 30¬∞ ‚Äî especially approaching 90¬∞ ‚Äî energy transfers backward. Droplets deform, flatten, and eject aerosol spray in all directions. Standard urinals create exactly these conditions for the vast majority of users.

Once geometry has redirected 98%+ of fluid away from the user, the surface coating handles the rest. Standard porcelain is actually hydrophilic ‚Äî it attracts and holds water. Urine spreads, sits, and breaks down into ammonia. That's where the smell comes from.

Standard hydrophobic coatings (inspired by the lotus leaf) create microscopic air cushions that repel water. They partially work ‚Äî but a high-pressure stream can collapse the air layer permanently. Surfactants in urine further degrade the chemistry. One scratch from a cleaning brush destroys it.

Our solution draws from the pitcher plant (Nepenthes). Instead of trapping air, it traps a thin layer of liquid lubricant in its surface pores ‚Äî creating a near-frictionless interface that causes insects to slide helplessly into its digestive chamber. The liquid layer is self-renewing and cannot be permanently displaced.

SLIPS (Slippery Liquid-Infused Porous Surfaces) replicates this with a perfluorinated lubricant locked into nano-porous ceramic. Applied via kiln-aspiration and thermal curing ‚Äî permanently baked into the fixture. Resistant to bleach, descaling acids, steam cleaning, and 40‚Äì70 abrasion cycles. If scratched, the lubricant flows in and self-heals.
